Australia's Threatened Species

The Australian Government's Department of Climate Change, Energy, the Environment, and Water (DCCEW) published the Threatened Species Action Plan (TSAP) 2022-2032 in 2022, detailling their strategies for the recovery of a number of threatened species across Australia. This map visualises areas where these species, known as priority species, had been observed or were known to be likely to occur as of 2022.

The Threatened Species Index (TSX) is a TERN project combining monitoring data from over 230 different sources. This map shows the centres of IBRA subregions where monitoring sites were located- these regions are classified based on common factors like climate, geography, and vegetation.

Each monitoring site records data for one threatened species, these species can be classified by taxonomic group into amphibians, birds, mammals and plants. It can be seen that birds are the most common taxonomic group to be monitored, with mammals as the second most common and amphibians and plants roughly equal.

Many of the areas on the TSAP map contain multiple species. The heatmap above shows how often the most common 20 species occur together with each other. White squares indicate when species never occurred together. Along the diagonal, co-occurrences of species with themselves indicate how many total areas they were observed in.

The TSX collates records of abundance for each species to determine the change in their population over time. Data for each year represents the change in population since the last year, with the reference value 1 being the abundance in the first recorded year, 1985.

It can be observed that amphibians experienced the most rapid decline in population. While the other taxonomic groups largely only declined in population, mammals actually experienced some reasonable increases in the early year. Despite this, populations of all species reduced by as much as 40% of their original values by 2021.
